The Project

When designing our new course, The AI Architect (currently in Early Access), my team and I created an unconventional and expensive initiative: Build to Refund.

The rules are simple: As long as you follow the course, complete a project (Build), and share it on your social media (Share), we will fully refund (Refund) your tuition for the AI Architect course.





Update: Originally, this project was set to end on January 1, 2026. However, our backend data shows that students had more time and motivation to learn during Thanksgiving. Recognizing that the upcoming daily grind might slow down progress, we want to align with your Christmas and New Year holidays. Therefore, both the Early Access discount and the "Build to Refund" deadline are extended to February 1, 2026.

The Purpose

On the surface, this looks like a marketing tactic—trading tuition fees for traffic. But if that were the goal, the math wouldn't add up. First, the total traffic generated might not be massive. Second, refunding $50 or even 50% would show plenty of sincerity. There is no logical business reason to refund 100%—especially when we still cover the platform and payment processing fees.

I chose to do this because I want to use this mechanism to give you a push. I want to help you overcome two major psychological hurdles: the false satisfaction of learning without doing and the burden of perfectionism that prevents sharing.

I am offering a 100% refund because crossing these two thresholds is critical. I want to max out your motivation. This is also a benefit for our returning alumni. We may open this to new students in the future, but likely with a much smaller refund offer.

1. From Learner to Builder

In the AI era, the cost of acquiring knowledge is near zero. Watching a hundred videos on Prompt Engineering or listening to countless podcasts about Agents can create a hallucination of competence—"I get it."

This is not only useless; it is harmful. It gives you a dopamine hit, substituting action with passive consumption. As we emphasize in the course: AI is a craft; it is like swimming. You cannot learn to swim without getting in the water.

The primary intent of "Build to Refund" is mandatory delivery. I don't need you to build perfect commercial software. I need you to get your hands dirty and build muscle memory. The intuition you gain from staying up late to fix an API error, or watching an Agent you wrote successfully complete a workflow for the first time, is worth more than watching 10,000 tutorials.

Only what you Build is truly yours.

2. Overcoming "The Burden of Perfectionism" for Career Signaling

During interviews with past students, I discovered a deeper issue. Many students gave the course high ratings but remained very quiet. I assumed they weren't building anything. However, I found that almost all of them had engaged in deep practice and built impressive tools—they just hadn't shared them.

When I dug deeper, I found that everyone felt "sharing what I made/learned" was a heavy burden:





"Am I just doing free advertising? Do I look like a spammer?"



"What I built is so simple; will people look down on me if I post it?"

The first concern is easy to solve: focus on providing value. The second is a mindset that is hard to overcome. I went through this when I started creating content. Even after doing it for a long time, I hesitated to share content related to my professional expertise.

This usually stems from three mindsets:





Fear of Judgment ("I'm not good enough"): You worry about your code being messy or your project being too simple compared to "experts." This is the classic Imposter Syndrome, fueled by social media comparison.



The Perfectionism Trap ("I'm not ready yet"): You want to optimize the UI or clean up the code just one more time. As author Jodi Picoult said, "You can edit a bad page, but you can't edit a blank page." Perfectionism stops you from hitting "publish."



Fear of Self-Promotion ("I'm showing off"): Particularly in certain cultures, we are taught to be humble. We confuse "sharing value" with "bragging."

I call this "The Burden of Perfectionism" (or Elite Shame). We are too used to showing only perfect results and are ashamed to show the "process of effort."

The Reality: In the new job market, the Resume is depreciating, while the Portfolio is appreciating. Sharing your projects and insights builds your portfolio and increases your surface area for luck. It is high-value Career Signaling.

By sharing, you signal three things to your network:





Growth Mindset: I am not insecure; I am learning and growing publicly.



Hands-on & Tech-Forward: I don't just talk about AI; I have the initiative and ability to use it.



Problem Solver: I am thinking about how to use AI to solve actual problems.

This is Building in Public. In this era, working behind closed doors is dangerous. You need to let opportunity find you.

3. How to "Career Signal" with Class

If you decide to post, how do you do it in a way that is "high-level" and beneficial? Even if it helps promote the course, I don't want you to post hard ads. I want you to write a "Micro-Technical Report."

Principle 1: Show, Don't Tell Don't say, "This course is great, I learned AI." Say, "This document process used to take 3 hours. Over the weekend, I used Cursor + Builder Space to build a tool that compressed it to 5 minutes. Here were the challenges I faced, how I solved them, and what I plan to do next." Show your thinking, your struggle, and even your bugs. The real process is more moving than a perfect result.

Principle 2: Imply Competence, Don't Brag Focus on the Problem and the Solution. When you objectively describe how you solved a tricky logic problem, people in the know will instantly perceive your technical depth and business understanding. (Tip: Use AI to remove "fluff" from your writing).

Principle 3: Under 30 Minutes A portfolio isn't built in a day; it's built over a thousand days. Make sharing easy. Don't wait to release a "masterpiece." Grab an insight and finish the sharing action within 30 minutes. Worst case? No one cares. Best case? You get market feedback to guide your next step. "Scrappy and Fast" is the essence here.
